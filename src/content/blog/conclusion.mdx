## Executive Summary

The central claim of this series is that artificial intelligence in financial services has entered a recursive condition: the same machine-learning systems that generate the regulated risk surface are now the only feasible substrate on which the resulting compliance burden can be discharged. This is the Recursive Compliance Dynamic. It is not a rhetorical figure. It is a budgetary and operational fact, visible in the staffing models of compliance functions, in the architecture of supervisory technology, and in the textual demands of three regulatory instruments that together govern the sector in 2026. The EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689), the Digital Operational Resilience Act (Regulation 2022/2554), and the General Data Protection Regulation impose, jointly, a documentation, monitoring, and erasure load that no human-only compliance function can absorb at scale. The institutions that recognise this and invest accordingly will define the supervisory posture of the next decade; the institutions that do not will discover, by August 2026, that they have already been overtaken.

Part 1 establishes the conditions under which this recursion has emerged. Rule-based heuristics, which dominated AML and credit-decisioning workflows for three decades, fail in the non-stationary statistical environment of contemporary financial markets, in the high-dimensional feature space generated by modern data infrastructure, and in the bounded-rationality constraint imposed by human investigators. The asymmetric cost of false negatives (systemic, criminal, and supervisory) versus false positives (investigative cost and customer friction) creates a calibration dilemma that no Boolean rule system can resolve. The DORA regulation, which became applicable from 17 January 2025, and the AI Act, whose high-risk obligations apply from 2 August 2026, intensify this dilemma by demanding documentation, monitoring, and resilience guarantees that scale only through automation.

Part 2 examines two architectural responses that have moved from the academic literature into production deployment during 2024 and 2025. Heterogeneous Graph Transformers operate on the financial transaction graph as a typed, relational object, learning representations that capture the topological signatures of fraud rings and the meta-relational structure of typological knowledge. Variational Autoencoders, applied to high-frequency market data, learn an unsupervised generative model of normal trading behaviour against which manipulation patterns can be detected as reconstruction-error anomalies. Both architectures provide audit-relevant artefacts (attention traces, latent-space neighbourhoods) that begin to align with the AI Act's documentation and post-market monitoring obligations.

Part 3 addresses the limits of this alignment. The architectures' outputs are formally inspectable but not, in any strict sense, explainable: post-hoc attribution methods are themselves probabilistic and contestable, and attention-based explanations have been shown in the literature to be unreliable. The fairness obligations of the AI Act collide with the formal impossibility theorem of Kleinberg, Mullainathan, and Raghavan (2017), which proves that calibration, demographic parity, and equalised odds cannot in general be jointly satisfied. The right to erasure under GDPR Article 17 confronts the topological coupling problem in graph neural networks, where personal data persists not only in the learned parameters but in the message-passing-induced representations of neighbouring nodes. Verifiable unlearning in such architectures is an open research problem.

## Conclusion: The Recursion as Permanent Condition

The empirical and architectural arguments developed across the three parts converge on a single conclusion: the recursion is not a transitional artefact of an immature regulatory regime, and it will not be resolved by better engineering. It is the permanent condition under which AI-mediated financial services will operate. Two implications follow.

The first is institutional. Compliance functions in 2026 are not, principally, departments of lawyers and process auditors. They are interdisciplinary technical teams responsible for the monitoring infrastructure, the fairness dashboards, the explainability harnesses, and the unlearning pipelines that observe and constrain the first-order machine-learning systems. The skill mix required to staff these functions, combining graph algorithms, causal inference, regulatory interpretation, and software systems engineering, is in short supply, and the institutions that have invested ahead of the August 2026 enforcement deadline will be advantaged relative to those that treat the deadline as a project completion date.

The second implication is epistemological. The opacity of the underlying models places a permanent ceiling on the auditor's certainty. A supervisor in 2026 cannot, in the strict sense, verify that a deployed credit-scoring system is fair, interpretable, and erasure-compliant. The supervisor can only verify that an audit infrastructure exists, that it is operational, that it produces the artefacts the regulation requires, and that the institution has documented the trade-offs it has made among the irreconcilable formal criteria. This is a substantive shift from the regulatory posture of the pre-AI era, and it imposes a different kind of trust relationship between institutions and supervisors, one mediated by second-order machine-learning systems rather than by first-order rule audits.

Whether this trust relationship is sustainable, and whether the recursion terminates at the second-order audit layer or generates further layers above it, are the central open questions of financial AI governance in the period beyond 2026. The argument advanced here is that they are the right questions to be asking, and that the institutions, regulators, and researchers who treat them as foundational, rather than peripheral, will shape the direction of the discipline.
