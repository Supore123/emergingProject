## 1.1 The Application Domain: Financial Services as a Computational Surface

Financial services in 2026 no longer resemble the discrete, ledger-bound institutions of the late twentieth century. Retail banking, payments, market-making, insurance underwriting, and anti-money-laundering (AML) workflows now sit on a thick layer of probabilistic systems: gradient-boosted decision trees screening loan applications, transformer-based language models summarising suspicious activity reports, and graph neural networks parsing counterparty relationships across millions of accounts. The domain has effectively become a computational surface, where the speed and volume of decisions have outpaced the cognitive bandwidth of human analysts by several orders of magnitude.

The European Banking Authority (2025), in its November 2025 mapping of AI Act implications for the banking sector, identified credit scoring, AML triage, and operational risk modelling as the three primary surfaces on which supervisory attention will concentrate during the 2026 to 2027 period. The Bank of England's March 2026 Financial Stability Report further warned that algorithmic concentration risk — the dependence of multiple Tier 1 institutions on the same handful of foundation-model providers — has itself become a systemic concern (Bank of England, 2026). These surfaces are not neutral: they are the places where statistical inference touches statutory rights, and where the failure mode of a model translates directly into a fundamental-rights harm to a natural person.

## 1.2 Current Challenges: Non-Stationarity and the Failure of Heuristics

Rule-based heuristics dominated compliance technology for roughly three decades. A typical AML transaction-monitoring system in the 2010s was little more than a Boolean expression library: thresholds, jurisdictions, and velocity patterns triggered alerts. These systems were attractive to supervisors because they were legible: every alert traced to a rule, every rule to a typology, every typology to a Financial Action Task Force recommendation.

Three structural problems have rendered this approach untenable. First, the data-generating process is non-stationary; statistical properties drift continuously as adversaries adapt and the underlying economy shifts, so a rule calibrated against 2022 typologies is, by 2025, detecting a fossil pattern. Second, the dimensionality of the feature space has expanded beyond the reach of hand-crafted rules: device fingerprints, behavioural biometrics, graph topology, language content, and counterparty network centrality all matter simultaneously, and Boolean conjunctions over hundreds of weakly informative signals produce either trivial or vacuous decision boundaries. Third, and most consequentially, the rate at which suspicious activity reports can be reviewed by human investigators is bounded while the volume of triggering events is not. The bottleneck has migrated from detection to adjudication.

## 1.3 The Asymmetric Cost of AML Errors

The conventional binary classifier framework treats false positives and false negatives as symmetric. In AML, this symmetry collapses. A false negative — a laundering transaction passing through the screen — exposes the institution to enforcement action that may run into the hundreds of millions of euros, contributes to underlying predicate criminality (drug trafficking, sanctions evasion, human trafficking, terrorism financing), and degrades the integrity of the financial system. A false positive generates an investigation cost of tens of euros, an account freeze of finite duration, and a reputational cost mostly internalised by the institution.

Industry estimates published during 2025 suggested that more than 95 per cent of AML alerts close as false positives, that the marginal investigation cost sits between 30 and 70 euros per alert, and that the typical Tier 1 European bank carries an annual AML budget in the high nine figures (Hawk AI, 2025). The asymmetry creates a perverse calibration incentive: lowering the alerting threshold to satisfy supervisory expectations grows the false positive rate roughly linearly while the marginal cost stays constant, generating a budgetary load that crowds out genuine investigative work; raising the threshold deteriorates the recall figure reported to supervisors and exposes the institution to enforcement risk under the Sixth Anti-Money Laundering Directive and the AMLA Regulation that began phased application in 2025. The institution is caught between two failure modes that no rule-based system can jointly minimise.

The commercial reality of this asymmetry has driven the rise of next-generation RegTech providers. Hawk AI and Featurespace have built their current valuations entirely on replacing legacy Boolean rule engines with adaptive behavioural analytics, explicitly marketing their ability to slash the 95 per cent false-positive rate that plagues Tier 1 banks (Featurespace, 2025). Their market penetration proves that the shift away from static heuristics is no longer just an academic proposal; it is a budgetary necessity.

## 1.4 The Case for Machine Learning: The Recursive Compliance Dynamic

The orthodox counter-position to this algorithmic escalation is regulatory minimalism. Drawing on legal philosophy, most notably Hildebrandt's (2015) conception of 'legal protection by design', critics argue that the appropriate response to non-stationarity and asymmetric costs is not to build infinitely complex, opaque ML surveillance systems but to enforce structural market simplicity and accept a higher baseline of supervisory tolerance. If a rule-based system yields 95 per cent false positives, the minimalist argues, the solution is to reform the alerting threshold, not to abdicate the decision to an uninterpretable graph neural network.

Hildebrandt's (2015) position has genuine force. If the regulatory demand for near-zero false negatives is itself a policy choice rather than a logical necessity, then the recursive dynamic described here is not inevitable but manufactured — a political artefact dressed in mathematical clothing. The counter-argument, however, is that the asymmetry of underlying harms makes the zero-tolerance posture structurally inescapable. Sanctions evasion, human trafficking, and terrorism financing do not produce harms that are commensurable with the civil-liberties cost of account freezes; they produce harms of categorically different magnitude and irreversibility. The minimalist position assumes a symmetry of costs that does not exist, and so collapses into an argument that supervisors and electorates have already, repeatedly, refused to accept.

Two regulatory instruments, the EU AI Act (European Parliament and Council, 2024a, in force from 1 August 2024) and the Digital Operational Resilience Act (European Parliament and Council, 2022, applicable from 17 January 2025), have decisively reshaped the calculus. The AI Act's prohibited practices apply from 2 February 2025, governance and general-purpose AI obligations from 2 August 2025, and high-risk obligations from 2 August 2026. Credit scoring, loan approval, and AI-driven assessments affecting access to financial services are explicitly classified as high-risk under Annex III. DORA addresses operational resilience: it ensures that banks, insurance companies, and investment firms can withstand, respond to, and recover from ICT disruptions, with the European Supervisory Authorities having designated 19 critical ICT third-party providers in November 2025 (European Banking Authority, 2025).

These two regimes, alongside the GDPR's Article 17 right to erasure (European Parliament and Council, 2016), generate a documentation, monitoring, and incident-reporting load no human-only compliance function can absorb at scale. The core argument across this report is that the same machine-learning techniques that create the regulated risk surface — probabilistic credit decisions, automated AML triage, algorithmic market surveillance — are also the only feasible substrate on which the resulting compliance burden can be discharged. This is the Recursive Compliance Dynamic: AI generates the regulatory load, and AI is required to manage it. The recursion is not a rhetorical flourish; it is a budgetary and operational fact.
