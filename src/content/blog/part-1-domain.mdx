## Executive Summary

The financial industry has entered what this essay terms the **recursive compliance dynamic**: the phenomenon whereby the very regulatory instruments designed to govern algorithmic risk — MiFID II's transaction-reporting obligations, the EU AI Act's conformity assessments for high-risk systems, DORA's ICT resilience mandates — generate compliance workloads of such volume and complexity that institutions can only discharge them by deploying further AI. A regulated AI system must now be overseen by an auditing AI, whose own decision logic must itself be auditable. The recursion is not merely metaphorical; it has direct engineering consequences that will define the architecture of financial infrastructure for the next decade.

This blog concentrates that argument through a narrow but precisely chosen aperture: **Anti-Money Laundering (AML) and systemic fraud detection**. These two domains sit at the sharpest intersection of technical difficulty and regulatory exposure. AML obligations under the Sixth Anti-Money Laundering Directive (6AMLD) and the forthcoming EU AMLA supervisory framework impose criminal liability on compliance officers for inadequate screening; simultaneously, the EU AI Act classifies AML and credit-scoring systems as high-risk, triggering conformity assessment obligations that are practically impossible to discharge without the interpretability mechanisms that frontier ML models conspicuously lack.

The essay proceeds in three movements. Part 1 establishes why the domain's complexity renders classical rule-based systems structurally inadequate. Part 2 provides a technical anatomy of the two ML architectures most consequentially deployed in this space — Graph Neural Networks and anomaly detection ensembles — and examines precisely *how*, not merely *that*, they work. Part 3 confronts the deeper epistemological and political tensions: the irreducible conflict between model opacity and the EU AI Act's requirement for "auditable inference traces," the formal impossibility of simultaneously satisfying competing mathematical definitions of fairness, and what Cynthia Rudin's work implies about the industry's post-hoc explainability reflex. The conclusion argues that the defining constraint on financial AI will be the **asymmetry between model complexity and regulatory legibility** — and that resolving it demands not better explanations of opaque models, but a fundamental rethinking of what kinds of models financial institutions are permitted to deploy.

## Part 1: The Domain, Its Challenges, and the Case for Machine Learning

### 1.1 The Operational Reality of Financial Crime

Global money laundering flows are estimated by the United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime at between 2% and 5% of world GDP annually — figures that conceal enormous methodological uncertainty but are large enough to anchor the intuition that financial crime represents a first-order systemic risk, not merely a compliance inconvenience. The more operationally relevant number is the detection rate: the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) estimates that less than 1% of illicit funds are seized globally, a figure that has remained stubbornly stable despite three decades of escalating compliance expenditure.

This failure is architectural. The dominant paradigm for most of that period was **rules-based transaction monitoring (TM)**: deterministic threshold conditions, hand-crafted by compliance analysts, evaluated against individual transactions in isolation. A simplified but representative rule might read: *flag any cash deposit exceeding €9,500 that is followed within 48 hours by a wire transfer to a jurisdiction on the FATF grey list*. The intellectual genealogy of such rules traces back to the Bank Secrecy Act of 1970 and the conceptual apparatus of "structuring" — the deliberate decomposition of reportable transactions into sub-threshold amounts.

The limitations of this paradigm are not subtle.

**Adversarial adaptability.** Rules-based systems operate on the implicit assumption that the adversary's behaviour is stationary. It is not. Money laundering typologies evolve in direct response to enforcement patterns, a dynamic that criminologists term the "displacement effect." Once regulators publish guidance on structuring detection thresholds (as FinCEN does via its Suspicious Activity Report analytics), sophisticated criminal networks update their transaction architectures accordingly. The result is an arms race in which rule-writers are structurally disadvantaged: they can only encode patterns they have already observed, while adversaries can generate novel patterns at will. This is not a problem that can be solved by adding more rules; it is a fundamental limitation of the paradigm.

**The curse of dimensionality and feature engineering brittleness.** A transaction monitoring system for a mid-sized European retail bank typically evaluates several hundred features per transaction — amounts, counterparty jurisdictions, time-of-day distributions, account age, prior flagging history, product type, channel. A hand-crafted rule can incorporate perhaps three or four of these features before the combinatorial logic becomes unmanageable. The remaining feature space is simply ignored. This is not a resource constraint; it is an epistemological one. Analysts cannot perceive the high-dimensional correlation structures that distinguish legitimate from illegitimate behaviour when both are rare events distributed across dozens of variables.

**Non-stationarity of legitimate behaviour.** Equally problematic is the assumption that "normal" behaviour is stable. The COVID-19 pandemic produced dramatic shifts in transaction patterns — cash usage collapsed, e-commerce volumes surged, cross-border payments restructured — that invalidated years of baseline calibration virtually overnight. Rules calibrated on pre-2020 behaviour generated massive false positive spikes that overwhelmed compliance teams precisely when operational capacity was most constrained. The non-stationarity problem is not exceptional; macroeconomic shocks, geopolitical events, and product innovation all continuously perturb the distributional assumptions on which static rules rest.

### 1.2 The Asymmetric Cost of Classification Error

The central analytical frame for understanding why financial institutions have been slow to adopt more sophisticated detection methods — despite those methods demonstrably outperforming rules-based systems on recall and precision — is the **asymmetric cost structure** of misclassification.

In standard binary classification theory, we distinguish two error types: **false positives** (legitimate transactions flagged as suspicious) and **false negatives** (suspicious transactions that pass undetected). In most ML applications, the cost of these errors is roughly symmetric and calibration is a matter of choosing an operating point on the ROC curve that reflects business preferences.

Financial crime detection inverts this symmetry in a way that creates a systematic institutional bias toward over-alerting.

Consider the incentive structure. A false negative — a money laundering transaction that escapes detection — creates a *potential* regulatory liability. The exposure is probabilistic: the transaction must be detected by another party, investigated, linked back to the originating institution, and the institution must be found to have failed its AML obligations. Even then, the fines, while large in headline terms, represent a small fraction of total revenue for major institutions. The probability-weighted expected cost of a single false negative is, in most cases, small.

A false positive — a legitimate customer flagged and subject to an enhanced due diligence (EDD) investigation — creates *immediate, certain* costs. Compliance analyst time is expensive (senior AML analysts at tier-one banks command salaries of £80,000–£120,000 in London); each alert requires documented investigation; customer relationships are strained; in the worst case, accounts are exited at significant cost to the business relationship. The EBA's 2023 thematic review found that major European banks generated Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) volumes in which 90–98% of alerts were ultimately determined to be false positives.

The practical consequence is that financial institutions have historically calibrated their TM systems to **very low recall thresholds** — flagging a large fraction of the transaction volume rather than risking a missed detection — which in turn produces the alert avalanche that makes meaningful investigation impossible. The system collapses under its own false positive burden, achieving neither genuine detection nor operational efficiency.

This is the core case for ML adoption: not merely that ML systems achieve higher recall at equivalent precision, but that they allow institutions to **navigate the cost asymmetry** by constructing risk scores that can be calibrated with far greater granularity than binary rule thresholds permit. A GNN-derived risk score of 0.73 for a given transaction cluster carries very different investigative implications than a binary "ALERT/NO ALERT" signal, and creates an audit trail — a probability estimate with attributable features — that is itself a compliance artefact.

### 1.3 The Regulatory Architecture and Its Demands on AI

The regulatory environment has not merely permitted ML adoption; it has effectively mandated it by setting compliance obligations that are computationally intractable without algorithmic support.

**MiFID II and MAR transaction reporting.** The Markets in Financial Instruments Directive II, in force since January 2018, requires investment firms to report up to 65 data fields per transaction to national competent authorities within the T+1 reporting window. Across the EU's major exchanges, this generates hundreds of millions of reportable events daily. The Market Abuse Regulation (MAR) simultaneously requires firms to maintain "effective systems and procedures to detect and report suspicious orders and transactions." The data volumes involved render manual surveillance literally impossible; algorithmic surveillance is not an enhancement but a legal requirement.

**The EU AI Act's high-risk classification.** The EU AI Act, entering phased application from August 2024, classifies systems used in "creditworthiness assessment," "risk assessment and pricing in life and health insurance," and law enforcement applications (which national AML supervisors have argued encompasses AML screening) as high-risk AI systems under Annex III. High-risk status triggers a suite of obligations — conformity assessment, registration in the EU database, ongoing monitoring, human oversight measures, and, critically, **transparency obligations requiring that the AI system's logic be interpretable to natural persons exercising oversight**. The tension between this requirement and the operational reality of deployed ML systems is the subject of Part 3.

**DORA and operational resilience.** The Digital Operational Resilience Act, applying from January 2025, imposes mandatory ICT risk management, incident reporting, and — most relevant to our argument — **third-party risk management** requirements that directly implicate AI vendors. An institution using a third-party AML AI system must now conduct regular audits of that system's operational resilience, maintain contractual rights of audit over the vendor's infrastructure, and demonstrate to supervisors that the third-party system's outputs can be replicated or substituted within defined recovery time objectives. This effectively prohibits the "black box as a service" model that has characterised early AI vendor relationships in compliance.

The composite picture is of a regulatory environment that simultaneously demands AI deployment (by setting compliance workloads that cannot be discharged manually), constrains AI architecture (by requiring interpretability and auditability), and penalises AI failure (through criminal and civil liability for compliance officers). This is the recursive compliance dynamic made concrete.
