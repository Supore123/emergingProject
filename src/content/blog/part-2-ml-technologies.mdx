## 2.1 Why Graph Structure Matters for Financial Crime

The transaction record of a modern bank is, formally, a directed multigraph. Nodes correspond to accounts, merchants, devices, and counterparties; edges correspond to transactions, ownership relationships, shared identifiers, and behavioural co-occurrences. The relational structure carries most of the predictive signal. A wire of 9,500 euros from a small business to a personal account is unremarkable in isolation; the same wire becomes informative when the recipient account has, in the prior week, received structurally similar deposits from twelve other small businesses, all of which share a registration address visible only at three hops of distance.

Tabular machine-learning models discard this structure by construction. They flatten each transaction into a feature vector in which graph-topological information must be hand-engineered into summary statistics: in-degree, out-degree, clustering coefficient, PageRank score. Hand-engineered features inherit all the brittleness of the rule-based systems they were intended to replace. They presume that the analyst already knows which topological motifs correspond to laundering, which is precisely the knowledge that a learning system is supposed to discover.

Graph neural networks (GNNs) address the representation problem by learning on the graph directly. The earliest variants, in particular the Graph Convolutional Network of Kipf and Welling (2017) and GraphSAGE of Hamilton et al. (2017), treated all edges and nodes as being of one type. This homogeneity assumption is unrealistic in financial data, where accounts, merchants, devices, and IP addresses participate in qualitatively different relations. The Heterogeneous Graph Transformer (HGT), introduced by Hu et al. in 2020, was designed precisely to relax that assumption.

## 2.2 Heterogeneous Graph Transformers: Architectural Mechanics

An HGT operates on a heterogeneous graph in which each node carries a type label drawn from a finite vocabulary (for example, account, merchant, device, counterparty, jurisdiction) and each edge carries a relation label (for example, transferred-to, registered-with, accessed-from, sanctioned-against). The architecture is a stack of attention layers, but unlike the homogeneous Graph Attention Network (GAT) of Veličković et al. (2018), the parameters of the attention mechanism are themselves indexed by the node-type and edge-type pair.

Concretely, for a target node t with type τ(t) and a source node s with type τ(s) connected by an edge of relation r, the attention coefficient is computed by projecting the source representation through a relation-specific key matrix and the target representation through a type-specific query matrix. The bilinear form that scores their compatibility is mediated by a relation-specific weight tensor. The aggregation step then weights messages from each neighbour by the resulting attention coefficient, applies a relation-specific value transformation, and combines the outcome with the target's prior representation through a type-specific feed-forward layer.

Three properties of this construction matter for fraud detection. First, the architecture preserves type information end-to-end. A "transfer" edge between two accounts is not collapsed into the same parameter set as a "shares-device" edge; the model can learn that the latter is a strong predictor of collusion while the former is a weak one. Second, attention is meta-relational: the model learns which relations are informative for which node types, which is itself a structural inductive bias well-matched to the typological taxonomies that AML investigators already use. Third, the attention weights are inspectable. For a flagged account, an investigator can extract the top-k most attentionally salient neighbours at each layer, providing a tractable substrate for the auditable inference traces that the EU AI Act is going to require from August 2026 onwards.

## 2.3 Detecting Fraud Rings: From Pairwise Anomaly to Topological Signature

A fraud ring is, by definition, a topological object rather than a transactional one. The classical "smurfing" pattern, in which a single beneficiary receives many sub-threshold deposits from a fan of accounts, was already approachable by rule-based systems because the topology was simple and known. Contemporary rings are more elaborate: they involve mule networks recruited through gig-economy platforms, layering through cryptocurrency on-ramps, integration through invoice fraud against legitimate businesses, and obfuscation through deliberately introduced noise transactions that mimic benign behaviour.

An HGT trained on a labelled subset of historical fraud rings learns to embed accounts in a representation space where ring members cluster densely. Detection then proceeds in two stages. First, a node-level classifier scores each account's posterior probability of belonging to a fraud ring. Second, a community-detection step (typically a variant of Louvain or Leiden modularity optimisation, applied to the embedding-induced similarity graph) groups high-scoring accounts into candidate rings. The output is not a single suspicious transaction but a candidate criminal organisation with a hypothesised topology, a feature representation per member, and an attention trace explaining why each member was included.

Empirical work published in 2025 reported F1 score improvements of approximately 4 to 5 percentage points over homogeneous GNN baselines on Ethereum Ponzi-detection benchmarks, alongside polynomial-time convergence guarantees and certified robustness bounds against adversarial perturbations of the graph. Comparable gains have been reported on the Elliptic++ Bitcoin transaction dataset, where heterogeneous attention captures the wallet-to-transaction type distinction that homogeneous architectures collapse.

The transition from pairwise anomaly detection to topological signatures is now actively deployed in production. Industry leaders in decision intelligence, most notably [Quantexa](https://www.quantexa.com/), have operationalised graph-based entity resolution at scale. Their platforms demonstrate that contextualising a transaction within its k-hop counterparty network is currently the only viable method for detecting the layered, multi-entity mule networks deployed by modern organised crime.

## 2.4 The Operational Constraints

Three operational constraints qualify the deployment of HGTs in production AML pipelines. First, the graph is dynamic. Accounts open and close, transactions arrive in continuous time, and a static snapshot is stale within minutes. Production deployments therefore use temporal extensions of HGT in which edges carry a time encoding (typically a sinusoidal embedding of a continuous timestamp), and the attention mechanism is masked to enforce causal ordering. The Spatial-Temporal-Aware Graph Transformer line of work, developed during 2023 to 2025, is the canonical reference. Second, scale is severe: a Tier 1 European bank holds on the order of hundreds of millions of accounts and tens of billions of transactions per year. Full-graph training is infeasible; production systems rely on neighbour sampling, with the sampling distribution itself becoming a hyperparameter that affects both detection performance and bias profile. Third, the graph contains personal data within the meaning of the General Data Protection Regulation. The right to erasure, which is examined in Part 3, has structural implications for how the model can be trained and updated.

## 2.5 Variational Autoencoders for Market Surveillance

Market surveillance, as distinct from AML, is the regulatory function that detects manipulation, insider trading, spoofing, and disorderly behaviour in trading venues. The detection problem has a different statistical character. The space of "normal" market behaviour is high-dimensional, vast, and difficult to enumerate; the space of "abnormal" behaviour is also large, but its instances are extremely rare and frequently novel. Supervised classification, which assumes a labelled corpus of representative anomalies, is therefore poorly suited to the problem. The dominant paradigm is unsupervised anomaly detection, and within that paradigm the Variational Autoencoder (VAE) has become the architectural default since approximately 2022.

A VAE is a generative model that learns to reconstruct its inputs through a probabilistic bottleneck. Mechanically, an encoder network maps an input x (in this context, a fixed-length window of order book or trade data) to the parameters of a Gaussian distribution over a latent variable z. A sample is drawn from this distribution and passed through a decoder network, which produces a reconstruction x̂. The training objective is the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO), which decomposes into a reconstruction term (the expected log-likelihood of x given z) and a regularisation term (the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the encoder's posterior and a standard Gaussian prior).

The use of the VAE for anomaly detection rests on a simple property: the model is trained on a large corpus of historical, presumed-benign market behaviour, and consequently it learns to reconstruct such behaviour with low error. When the model is presented with an input drawn from a behavioural pattern that is rare or absent in the training distribution, the reconstruction error rises. The reconstruction error itself, or a calibrated transformation of it (often the negative log-likelihood under the learned generative model), serves as the anomaly score.

For market surveillance, the input window typically encodes message-level order book activity: insertions, cancellations, and executions, time-stamped at microsecond resolution and aggregated into a tensor representation. Spoofing manifests as a pattern of large limit orders placed and cancelled before execution, generating a reconstruction signature that differs from genuine liquidity provision. Layering, momentum ignition, and quote stuffing each generate distinct reconstruction-error profiles. The advantage of the VAE relative to rule-based surveillance is that it does not require the regulator to enumerate the catalogue of manipulation typologies in advance.

This probabilistic approach is actively reshaping institutional surveillance. Market infrastructure providers, including the deep-learning integrations within [Nasdaq SMARTS](https://www.nasdaq.com/solutions/market-surveillance) surveillance suite (which monitors over 50 marketplaces globally), are increasingly relying on unsupervised anomaly detection. By doing so, they can catch zero-day manipulation tactics, such as novel momentum ignition strategies, before they can be codified into static rules.

## 2.6 The Probabilistic Bottleneck and Its Auditability Implications

The VAE's latent variable z carries a property that is consequential for compliance use cases: its dimensionality is small (typically 16 to 128), it is approximately disentangled, and it lies in a continuous space. This means that a flagged trading episode can be located in latent space, and its nearest neighbours can be retrieved. An investigator can ask the system not "is this anomalous?" but "what does this anomaly resemble, in the population of historical anomalies the model has previously flagged?". This is a substantively different epistemic posture from the binary alerting that characterises rule-based surveillance, and it aligns with the documentation and post-market monitoring obligations that the AI Act will impose on high-risk systems from August 2026.

A second property is consequential for adversarial robustness. The reconstruction-error score is, by construction, sensitive to inputs that lie far from the training distribution. An adversary who knows the architecture and approximate training distribution can attempt to craft manipulation patterns that lie close to the manifold, evading the surveillance system. Robust variants, including adversarially trained VAEs, denoising VAEs, and ensemble methods that aggregate reconstruction errors across multiple models trained with different latent dimensionalities, have been the subject of active research throughout 2024 and 2025. The arms race between manipulation strategies and detection architectures is structurally similar to the AML arms race, and the same Recursive Compliance Dynamic applies.

The architectural choices described here are not value-neutral. They embed assumptions about which features are informative, which neighbours are relevant, and which manifold corresponds to "normal" behaviour. The next part addresses the consequences of those embedded assumptions for fairness, transparency, and the right to erasure.
