## 2.1 Why Graph Structure Matters for Financial Crime

The transaction record of a modern bank is, formally, a directed multigraph: nodes correspond to accounts, merchants, devices, and counterparties; edges correspond to transactions, ownership relationships, shared identifiers, and behavioural co-occurrences. The relational structure carries most of the predictive signal. A wire of 9,500 euros from a small business to a personal account is unremarkable in isolation; the same wire becomes informative when the recipient account has, in the prior week, received structurally similar deposits from twelve other small businesses sharing a registration address visible only at three hops of distance.

Tabular machine-learning models discard this structure by construction. They flatten each transaction into a feature vector in which graph-topological information must be hand-engineered into summary statistics: in-degree, out-degree, clustering coefficient, PageRank score. Hand-engineered features inherit all the brittleness of the rule-based systems they were intended to replace; they presume the analyst already knows which topological motifs correspond to laundering, which is precisely the knowledge a learning system is supposed to discover. Graph neural networks (GNNs) address the representation problem by learning on the graph directly. The earliest variants, the Graph Convolutional Network (Kipf and Welling, 2017) and GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017), treated all edges and nodes as one type. This homogeneity assumption is unrealistic in financial data, where accounts, merchants, devices, and IP addresses participate in qualitatively different relations. The Heterogeneous Graph Transformer (HGT), introduced by Hu et al. (2020), was designed to relax that assumption.

## 2.2 Heterogeneous Graph Transformers: Architectural Mechanics

An HGT operates on a heterogeneous graph in which each node carries a type label drawn from a finite vocabulary (account, merchant, device, counterparty, jurisdiction) and each edge carries a relation label (transferred-to, registered-with, accessed-from, sanctioned-against). The architecture is a stack of attention layers, but unlike the homogeneous Graph Attention Network (Veličković et al., 2018), the parameters of the attention mechanism are themselves indexed by the node-type and edge-type pair.

Concretely, for a target node t with type τ(t) and a source node s with type τ(s) connected by an edge of relation r, the attention coefficient is computed by projecting the source representation through a relation-specific key matrix and the target representation through a type-specific query matrix. The bilinear form scoring their compatibility is mediated by a relation-specific weight tensor. The aggregation step then weights messages from each neighbour by the resulting attention coefficient, applies a relation-specific value transformation, and combines the outcome with the target's prior representation through a type-specific feed-forward layer.

Three properties of this construction matter for fraud detection. First, the architecture preserves type information end-to-end: a "transfer" edge between two accounts is not collapsed into the same parameter set as a "shares-device" edge, so the model can learn that the latter is a strong predictor of collusion while the former is weak. Second, attention is meta-relational: the model learns which relations are informative for which node types, which is itself a structural inductive bias well-matched to the typological taxonomies that AML investigators already use. Third, the attention weights are inspectable. For a flagged account, an investigator can extract the top-k most attentionally salient neighbours at each layer, providing a tractable substrate for the auditable inference traces the EU AI Act requires from August 2026.

## 2.3 Detecting Fraud Rings: From Pairwise Anomaly to Topological Signature

A fraud ring is a topological object rather than a transactional one. The classical "smurfing" pattern, in which a single beneficiary receives many sub-threshold deposits from a fan of accounts, was already approachable by rule-based systems because the topology was simple and known. Contemporary rings are more elaborate: mule networks recruited through gig-economy platforms, layering through cryptocurrency on-ramps, integration through invoice fraud against legitimate businesses, and obfuscation through deliberately introduced noise transactions that mimic benign behaviour.

An HGT trained on a labelled subset of historical fraud rings learns to embed accounts in a representation space where ring members cluster densely. Detection then proceeds in two stages. First, a node-level classifier scores each account's posterior probability of belonging to a fraud ring. Second, a community-detection step (typically Louvain or Leiden modularity optimisation applied to the embedding-induced similarity graph) groups high-scoring accounts into candidate rings. The output is not a single suspicious transaction but a candidate criminal organisation with a hypothesised topology, a feature representation per member, and an attention trace explaining inclusion. Empirical work published in 2025 reported F1 score improvements of approximately 4 to 5 percentage points over homogeneous GNN baselines on Ethereum Ponzi-detection benchmarks, alongside polynomial-time convergence guarantees and certified robustness bounds against adversarial graph perturbations. Comparable gains have been reported on the Elliptic++ Bitcoin transaction dataset, where heterogeneous attention captures the wallet-to-transaction type distinction that homogeneous architectures collapse.

The transition from pairwise anomaly detection to topological signatures is now actively deployed in production. Quantexa's contextual decision intelligence platform, deployed across seven of the top ten global banks as of 2025, processes entity graphs containing over one trillion relationship edges. In a published deployment at HSBC, the platform reduced false-positive AML alerts by approximately 60 per cent while increasing true-positive detection rates — a result that a tabular model operating on the same transaction data could not replicate, because the predictive signal resided entirely in the counterparty network topology (Quantexa, 2024). Contextualising a transaction within its k-hop counterparty network is currently the only viable method for detecting the layered, multi-entity mule networks deployed by modern organised crime.

## 2.4 Operational Constraints

Three operational constraints qualify the deployment of HGTs in production AML pipelines. First, the graph is dynamic: accounts open and close, transactions arrive in continuous time, and a static snapshot is stale within minutes. Production deployments use temporal extensions of HGT in which edges carry a time encoding (typically a sinusoidal embedding of a continuous timestamp), and the attention mechanism is masked to enforce causal ordering. Second, scale is severe: a Tier 1 European bank holds hundreds of millions of accounts and tens of billions of transactions per year, so full-graph training is infeasible and production systems rely on neighbour sampling, with the sampling distribution itself becoming a hyperparameter that affects detection performance and bias profile. Third, the graph contains personal data within the meaning of the GDPR (European Parliament and Council, 2016), and the right to erasure has structural implications for how the model can be trained and updated.

These three constraints are not independent. The sampling strategies required to manage scale introduce bias profiles that interact directly with the personal data governance obligations: the nodes under-sampled in training are often those belonging to minority demographic groups, precisely the groups whose fairness outcomes are most sensitive to representation in the training set. This creates a structural link between the operational choices of this section and the ethical obligations developed in Part 3.

## 2.5 Variational Autoencoders for Market Surveillance

Market surveillance, distinct from AML, detects manipulation, insider trading, spoofing, and disorderly behaviour in trading venues. The detection problem has a different statistical character: the space of normal market behaviour is high-dimensional, vast, and difficult to enumerate; the space of abnormal behaviour is also large but its instances are rare and frequently novel. Supervised classification, which assumes a labelled corpus of representative anomalies, is therefore poorly suited. The dominant paradigm is unsupervised anomaly detection, and within it the Variational Autoencoder (VAE) has become the architectural default since approximately 2022 (Kingma and Welling, 2014).

A VAE is a generative model that learns to reconstruct its inputs through a probabilistic bottleneck. An encoder network maps an input x (a fixed-length window of order book or trade data) to the parameters of a Gaussian distribution over a latent variable z. A sample is drawn and passed through a decoder, producing a reconstruction x̂. The training objective is the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO), which decomposes into a reconstruction term (the expected log-likelihood of x given z) and a regularisation term (the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the encoder's posterior and a standard Gaussian prior). The use of the VAE for anomaly detection rests on a simple property: trained on a corpus of historical, presumed-benign market behaviour, the model reconstructs such behaviour with low error; when presented with a behavioural pattern rare or absent in training, the reconstruction error rises, and that error serves as the anomaly score.

For market surveillance, the input window typically encodes message-level order book activity time-stamped at microsecond resolution. Spoofing manifests as a pattern of large limit orders placed and cancelled before execution, generating a reconstruction signature distinct from genuine liquidity provision; layering, momentum ignition, and quote stuffing each generate distinct profiles. The VAE's advantage over rule-based surveillance is that it does not require the regulator to enumerate manipulation typologies in advance. This probabilistic approach is reshaping institutional surveillance: deep-learning integrations within [Nasdaq SMARTS](https://www.nasdaq.com/solutions/market-surveillance), which monitors over fifty marketplaces globally, increasingly rely on unsupervised anomaly detection to catch zero-day manipulation tactics before they can be codified into static rules (Nasdaq, 2024).

## 2.6 The Probabilistic Bottleneck and Its Auditability Implications

The VAE's latent variable z is consequential for compliance: its dimensionality is small (typically 16 to 128), it is approximately disentangled, and it lies in a continuous space. A flagged trading episode can be located in latent space and its nearest neighbours retrieved. An investigator can ask not "is this anomalous?" but "what does this anomaly resemble in the population of historical anomalies the model has previously flagged?". This is a substantively different epistemic posture from the binary alerting that characterises rule-based surveillance, and it aligns with the documentation and post-market monitoring obligations the AI Act imposes on high-risk systems from August 2026.

A second property is consequential for adversarial robustness. The reconstruction-error score is sensitive to inputs lying far from the training distribution; an adversary who knows the architecture and approximate training distribution can attempt to craft manipulation patterns close to the manifold, evading detection. Robust variants, including adversarially trained VAEs, denoising VAEs, and ensemble methods aggregating reconstruction errors across models with different latent dimensionalities, have been the subject of active research throughout 2024 and 2025. The arms race between manipulation strategies and detection architectures is structurally similar to the AML arms race, and the same Recursive Compliance Dynamic applies.

The VAE paradigm is not without epistemological and architectural limits. Market-surveillance benchmarks suffer from acute labelling bias: the 'ground truth' of historical manipulation episodes is itself a product of prior regulator detection, so unsupervised models trained on these benchmarks partially learn to reproduce existing supervisory blind spots — a manifestation of Goodhart's Law (Goodhart, 1984) applied to market surveillance. Architecturally, VAEs have been partially displaced since 2024 by diffusion models and normalising flows, which offer more tractable exact-likelihood estimation and sharper generation of synthetic order-book states. Yet the core compliance argument holds regardless of generative architecture: the auditability properties of latent-space neighbourhood retrieval generalise, shifting the supervisory interaction from binary alerting to semantic, nearest-neighbour interpretation.
