## 3.1 The Opacity Problem and the Statutory Demand for Traces

Article 12 of the EU AI Act requires high-risk AI systems to maintain automated logging sufficient to enable a posteriori traceability of operation. Article 13 requires sufficient transparency to enable deployers to interpret and use system output appropriately. Article 86, applicable from 2 August 2026, introduces an individual right of explanation: any person subject to a decision based on a high-risk AI system that significantly affects them is entitled to a clear explanation of the system's role, the main parameters that influenced the output, and the human oversight involved (European Parliament and Council, 2024a). This right extends beyond the GDPR's Article 22 right concerning automated decision-making, which applies only to "solely automated" decisions.

The friction is structural. The architectures described in Part 2 are non-linear function approximators with parameter counts in the millions to hundreds of millions; their decisions emerge from a composition of attention coefficients, learned embeddings, and stochastic samples that admit no closed-form expression in terms of input features. The model does not, in any meaningful sense, "know why" it produced its output. Three families of post-hoc explanation techniques have been proposed: SHAP and LIME provide local feature attributions; integrated gradients and attention rollout provide gradient-based attributions; counterfactual explanations identify minimal input perturbations that would have flipped the decision. None constitutes an inference trace in the strict sense the AI Act appears to require. SHAP attributions carry their own sampling variance; attention coefficients have been shown unreliable as explanations because models with substantially different attention patterns can produce identical outputs (Jain and Wallace, 2019). The European Central Bank's January 2026 supervisory guidance acknowledged this gap explicitly, noting that "no currently available post-hoc explanation method satisfies the inference-trace standard required by Article 12" (European Central Bank, 2026, p. 14).

## 3.2 The Formal Impossibility of Joint Fairness

The fairness literature has converged on a small number of canonical statistical criteria. Demographic parity requires equal positive prediction rates across protected groups. Equalised odds requires equal true positive and false positive rates across groups. Calibration within groups requires that, for any predicted probability score, the empirical positive rate equals the score, separately within each group. A natural intuition holds that a sufficiently well-designed model should satisfy all three. Kleinberg, Mullainathan and Raghavan (2017), and independently Chouldechova (2017), proved that this intuition is wrong.

The result is a formal impossibility theorem. If the base rates of the positive class differ between groups — as they empirically do in essentially every applied fairness setting — any classifier satisfying calibration within groups must violate equalised odds, and conversely. The three criteria are jointly unsatisfiable in the general case. An institution deploying a credit-scoring model under the AI Act's Annex III high-risk regime must therefore choose, and the regulation is silent on which definition takes precedence.

The choice is consequential. A bank that prioritises calibration produces a model whose probability scores are honest, in the sense that a "30 per cent default risk" prediction corresponds to an empirical default rate of approximately 30 per cent across all groups, but will necessarily produce different false positive rates across groups: applicants from a group with lower base default rates will face proportionally more false rejections than applicants from a group with higher base default rates. A bank that prioritises equalised odds inverts the trade-off: error rates equalise but the probabilistic interpretation becomes group-dependent. Neither choice is morally neutral and neither can be evaded by better engineering. The supervisory framework operating from August 2026 will require institutions to document this choice, justify it, and submit it to scrutiny; the justification "we picked the metric that gave us the best accuracy" will not, in any plausible reading of the AI Act's fundamental rights impact assessment, be accepted.

Regulators are only beginning to confront this mathematical reality. The Monetary Authority of Singapore's (2022) Veritas Initiative attempted to operationalise fairness metrics for credit risk; its FEAT Principles assessment framework, applied across credit, insurance, and wealth-management use cases, found that no single institution in the pilot cohort could simultaneously satisfy demographic parity and calibration on its production scoring model, empirically confirming the Kleinberg result in a live regulatory context (Monetary Authority of Singapore, 2022). This formal impossibility is not merely a technical defect; it is the mathematical expression of value pluralism. Berlin (1969) and Williams (1981) long argued that fundamental human values are frequently incommensurable — that they cannot be mapped onto a single conflict-free metric without loss. By mandating fairness in Annex III systems without specifying a hierarchy of fairness definitions, the AI Act offloads a profound, unresolved question of liberal political philosophy onto the engineering function. The impossibility theorem reveals a structural fact about the regulation: it demands that data scientists mathematically resolve a moral tension that society itself has not yet settled.

A constructive institutional response is not to pick a metric in private but to publish the choice. Institutions should be required to file a Fairness Metric Declaration alongside their AI Act conformity assessment, specifying which criterion they have prioritised, what base-rate data informed the decision, and which groups bear the resulting distributional cost. Such a declaration would shift the regulatory conversation from a hidden technical compromise to a contestable political claim, restoring some of the democratic accountability that the impossibility theorem otherwise erodes.

## 3.3 Machine Unlearning in Graph Architectures: The GDPR Obligation Meets Topological Coupling

Article 17 of the GDPR confers on data subjects the right to obtain erasure of their personal data without undue delay (European Parliament and Council, 2016). The conventional reading treats erasure as a database operation: a row is removed, a backup purged, compliance certified. Where personal data has been used to train a machine-learning model, this reading is structurally insufficient: the model is itself a derived informational artefact of the training data, and the data subject's information persists in the learned parameters.

The field of machine unlearning, crystallised since approximately 2022, addresses this gap. Exact unlearning, achieved by retraining from scratch on the dataset minus erased records, is the gold standard but computationally prohibitive for large models. Approximate techniques, including SISA training, influence-function parameter updates, and gradient-ascent fine-tuning on the to-be-forgotten records, trade certified erasure for tractability. A 2025 development of note is the proposal of source-free unlearning methods, in which the institution applies a single Newton update to model parameters, calibrated by random noise, with performance comparable to full retraining.

For graph neural networks, unlearning is qualitatively harder than for tabular models because of topological coupling. When a data subject's account is removed, the edges connecting that account to its counterparties also carry information that has propagated through the message-passing layers into the representations of every neighbouring node; within k message-passing steps, the influence of the erased node has spread to its k-hop neighbourhood. Unlearning the node, in the strict sense of producing a model whose parameters are statistically indistinguishable from one trained without that node, requires undoing this propagation throughout the affected neighbourhood. Recent work, including subgraph federated unlearning frameworks proposed in 2025 and verifiable forgetting techniques developed in 2026 specifically for GNNs, has demonstrated meaningful progress, though the verifiability question — how a supervisor or data subject can confirm erasure has actually occurred — remains open and is one of the central technical questions on which the joint operation of GDPR Article 17 and AI Act Article 12 depends.

## 3.4 The Auditor's Predicament

Considered jointly, the obligations above place the supervisor in a difficult epistemic position. The auditor is asked to certify that a model is interpretable enough to satisfy Article 13, fair enough to satisfy the fundamental-rights impact assessment, and erasure-compliant enough to satisfy GDPR Article 17. Each test is contestable: the interpretability evidence is post-hoc and probabilistic; the fairness evidence requires a metric choice the regulation does not adjudicate; the erasure evidence requires verifying a counterfactual property of a model whose parameter space is too large to inspect directly.

A constructive response is not to abandon the regulatory project but to recognise that the burden of proof has shifted. Under the rule-based regime, the institution demonstrated compliance by exhibiting a rule and tracing it to a typology. Under the probabilistic regime, the institution must exhibit an audit infrastructure: a continuous monitoring system that logs decisions, a fairness dashboard tracking group-level error rates, an unlearning pipeline that responds to erasure requests within bounded latency, and a model card documenting the choices and trade-offs made. The Recursive Compliance Dynamic has already birthed a secondary industry: AI governance platforms like Credo AI and Holistic AI are scaling rapidly to provide exactly this audit infrastructure (Credo AI, 2025), translating the opacity of GNNs and VAEs into the continuous fairness dashboards and risk logs the AI Act requires. The audit infrastructure is itself a machine-learning artefact: second-order software observing first-order software, mandated by the regulations whose burden it is intended to discharge.

This is the Recursive Compliance Dynamic at its sharpest. The opacity of the underlying model creates the demand for an auditing apparatus, and that apparatus is itself constructed from machine-learning components whose own opacity will, in time, become the next layer of the recursion. Whether this regress can be terminated, or whether it merely defers the underlying epistemic problem one level up, is the central unresolved question of AI governance in financial services in 2026.
