## 3.1 The Opacity Problem and the Statutory Demand for Traces

Article 12 of the EU AI Act requires high-risk AI systems to maintain automated logging sufficient to enable a posteriori traceability of their operation. Article 13 requires that they be designed to be sufficiently transparent to enable deployers to interpret and use their output appropriately. Article 86, which becomes applicable from 2 August 2026, introduces an individual right of explanation: any person subject to a decision based on a high-risk AI system that significantly affects them is entitled to a clear explanation of the system's role in the decision-making process, the main parameters that influenced the output, and the human oversight involved in reaching the final decision. This right extends beyond the General Data Protection Regulation's Article 22 right concerning automated decision-making, which applies only to "solely automated" decisions.

The friction is structural. The architectures described in Part 2, the Heterogeneous Graph Transformer and the Variational Autoencoder, are non-linear function approximators with parameter counts in the millions to hundreds of millions. Their decisions emerge from a composition of attention coefficients, learned embeddings, and stochastic samples that admit no closed-form expression in terms of the input features. The model, in any meaningful sense, does not "know why" it produced its output, because the output is a fixed point of a long computational graph rather than the conclusion of a deductive argument.

Three families of post-hoc explanation techniques have been proposed to bridge the gap. SHAP and LIME provide local feature-attribution scores; integrated gradients and attention-rollout techniques provide gradient-based attributions; counterfactual explanations identify minimal perturbations of the input that would have flipped the decision. Each is a useful diagnostic. None constitutes an inference trace in the strict sense the AI Act appears to require. SHAP attributions are themselves derived from a sampling procedure and carry their own variance; attention coefficients in transformers have been shown, by Jain and Wallace (2019) and subsequent work, to be unreliable as explanations because models with substantially different attention patterns can produce identical outputs. The supervisory authority, in 2026, will be presented with auditable artefacts that are themselves probabilistic and contestable, and the question of which kind of trace satisfies Article 12 is, at the time of writing, unresolved at the level of harmonised standards.

## 3.2 The Formal Impossibility of Joint Fairness

The fairness literature has converged on a small number of canonical statistical criteria. Demographic parity requires that the positive prediction rate be equal across protected groups. Equalised odds requires that the true positive rate and false positive rate be equal across groups. Calibration within groups requires that, for any predicted probability score, the empirical positive rate equal the score, separately within each group. A natural intuition holds that a sufficiently well-designed model should satisfy all three. Kleinberg, Mullainathan and Raghavan (2017), and independently Chouldechova (2017), proved that this intuition is wrong.

The result is a formal impossibility theorem. If the base rates of the positive class differ between groups (which, in essentially every applied fairness setting, they do), then any classifier that satisfies calibration within groups must violate equalised odds, and conversely. The three criteria are, in the general case, jointly unsatisfiable. The institution deploying a credit-scoring model under the AI Act's Annex III high-risk regime must therefore make a choice. It cannot satisfy all reasonable formal definitions of fairness simultaneously, and the regulation is silent on which definition takes precedence.

The choice is consequential. A bank that prioritises calibration is producing a model whose probability scores are honest, in the sense that a "30 per cent default risk" prediction corresponds to an empirical default rate of approximately 30 per cent across all groups. The same bank, under the impossibility theorem, will necessarily produce different false positive rates across groups: applicants from a group with lower base default rates will face proportionally more false rejections than applicants from a group with higher base default rates. A bank that prioritises equalised odds inverts the trade-off: error rates are equal, but the probabilistic interpretation of the score becomes group-dependent.

Neither choice is morally neutral and neither choice can be evaded by better engineering. The supervisory framework that will operate from August 2026 onwards will require institutions to document this choice, justify it, and submit it to scrutiny. The justification that "we picked the metric that gave us the best accuracy" will not, in any plausible reading of the AI Act's fundamental rights impact assessment requirement, be accepted. Yet there is no neutral ground from which the choice can be made: the institution is being asked to allocate a moral cost between groups, and the regulation provides no metaethical scaffolding for doing so.

## 3.3 Machine Unlearning in Graph Architectures: The GDPR Obligation Meets Topological Coupling

Article 17 of the GDPR confers on data subjects the right to obtain erasure of their personal data without undue delay. The conventional reading of this provision treats erasure as a database operation: a row is removed, a backup is purged, and the institution certifies compliance. Where personal data has been used to train a machine-learning model, this reading is structurally insufficient. The model is itself a derived informational artefact of the training data, and the data subject's information, in a meaningful sense, persists in the learned parameters.

The field of machine unlearning, which has crystallised since approximately 2022, addresses this gap. Exact unlearning, achieved by retraining the model from scratch on the dataset minus the erased records, is the gold standard but is computationally prohibitive for large models. Approximate unlearning techniques, including SISA training, influence-function-based parameter updates, and gradient-ascent fine-tuning on the to-be-forgotten records, offer partial solutions that trade certified erasure for tractability. A 2025 development of note is the proposal of source-free unlearning methods, in which the institution applies a single Newton update to the model parameters, calibrated by random noise, and demonstrates performance comparable to full retraining.

For graph neural networks, the unlearning problem is qualitatively harder than for tabular models. The reason is topological coupling. When a data subject's account is removed, the edges connecting that account to its counterparties also carry information that has propagated through the message-passing layers into the representations of every neighbouring node. Within k message-passing steps, the influence of the erased node has spread to its k-hop neighbourhood. Unlearning the node, in the strict sense of producing a model whose parameters are statistically indistinguishable from one trained without that node, requires undoing this propagation throughout the affected neighbourhood. Recent work, including subgraph federated unlearning frameworks proposed in 2025 and verifiable forgetting techniques developed in 2026 specifically for GNNs, has demonstrated meaningful progress. The verifiability question, that is, the question of how a supervisor or data subject can confirm that erasure has actually occurred, remains open and is one of the central technical questions on which the joint operation of GDPR Article 17 and AI Act Article 12 depends.

## 3.4 The Auditor's Predicament

Considered jointly, the obligations described above place the supervisor in a difficult epistemic position. The auditor is asked to certify that a model is interpretable enough to satisfy Article 13, fair enough to satisfy the fundamental-rights impact assessment, and erasure-compliant enough to satisfy GDPR Article 17. Each of these tests is, individually, contestable. The interpretability evidence is post-hoc and probabilistic. The fairness evidence requires a metric choice that the regulation does not adjudicate. The erasure evidence requires verifying a counterfactual property of a model whose parameter space is too large to inspect directly.

A constructive response to this predicament is not to abandon the regulatory project but to recognise that the burden of proof has shifted. Under the rule-based regime, the institution demonstrated compliance by exhibiting a rule and tracing it to a typology. Under the probabilistic regime, the institution must demonstrate compliance by exhibiting an audit infrastructure: a continuous monitoring system that logs decisions, a fairness dashboard that tracks group-level error rates over time, an unlearning pipeline that responds to erasure requests within a bounded latency, and a model card that documents the choices and trade-offs that were made. The audit infrastructure is itself a machine-learning artefact. It is the second-order software that observes the first-order software, and its deployment is mandated by the regulations whose burden it is intended to discharge.

This is the Recursive Compliance Dynamic at its sharpest. The opacity of the underlying model creates the demand for an auditing apparatus, and that apparatus is itself constructed from machine-learning components whose own opacity will, in time, become the next layer of the recursion. Whether this regress can be terminated, or whether it merely defers the underlying epistemic problem one level up, is the central unresolved question of AI governance in financial services in 2026.
